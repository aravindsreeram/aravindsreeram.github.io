Sample Characteristics Tables & Comparisons of Means

Real-world data from an institutional gastric cancer dataset of 398 patients post-chemotherapy. Patient characteristics are doubly-stratified, first by Lauren pathologic type then by dichotomized lymph node ratio (high vs. low).

A common next step would be to compare low LNR vs. high LNR groups in the same dataset and test for statistical differences in means and distributions:

Logistic Regression Model

To search for variables that significantly increase or decrease the likelihood of a binary outcome, logistic regression models are used. For example, I've defined a high lymph node ratio (LNR; # metastatic nodes / total resected nodes) = 0.07 in my dataset of 390 gastric cancer patients. By running a logistic regression model, I discovered that diffuse and mixed subtypes (compared to intestinal), as well as poor chemotherapy response as shown by tumor regression grade, were predictors of having high LNR.
It is important to keep in mind that in this case, a continuous variable was dichotomized for demonstrative purposes. In reality, continuous variables are better suited for linear regression models, which follow an extremely similar format and as such I've chosen not to separately showcase here.

Univariate and Multivariate Regression Models of Survival

To assess the predictive value of a single variable on survival, univariate analyses can be used. In the model below, I evaluated the impact of several variables on survival in gastric cancer patients after systemic treatment. The multivariate regression shows that while variables like tumor regression grade (TRG) and age seemed to be significant on univariate analysis, they were kicked out of significance when accounting for covariates. This is an important distinction when trying to identify true associations to subsequently test for causation in prospective studies.

Kaplan-Meier Survival Curves with Log-Rank Test

The Kaplan-Meier estimator is a common non-parametric statistic employed in survival analysis. In assessing oncologic outcomes, time-to-event data (e.g. whether a death occurs and, if so, when) can be represented through survival curves like the ones below. Here, I am comparing the overall survival of node-positive vs. node-negative gastric cancer patients and employing a logrank test to confirm a statistically significant difference (p < 0.001).

Receiver Operating Characteristics Curve for Model Fit Accuracy

One method to compare two models is by generating a receiver operating characteristics (ROC) curve. The higher the area under this curve (AUC), the more accurate a univariate model at predicting a binary endpoint. Below, I have implemented a time-dependent ROC to evaluate how accurate lymph node ratio (LNR; # metastatic nodes / total resected nodes) versus purely # of metastatic (positive) lymph nodes are in predicting time-to-event data (survival outcomes). The results were statistically significant (p = 0.007; AUC 66.6 vs. 71.2), indicating that LNR was significantly a better predictor of survival than # metastatic nodes.
